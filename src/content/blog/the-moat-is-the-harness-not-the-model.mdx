A lot of things have happend in AI and Agentic AI since the release of ChatGPT 3.5 in end of 2022. First we had a wave of "ChatGPT" wrappers for various domains using "prompt engineering" and "RAG" to tailor for various domains.
This was mainly systems using stacked LLM calls for doing things, then people started to evaluate agentic loops and agentic workflows for e.g. legal tasks. In the midst of this people started to talk about "context engineering" instead of "prompt engineering" and "agentic harness" instead of "agentic loop". 
The idea is that the LLM is just one component in a larger system that includes tool use, memory, guardrails, and workflow integration. The real value is not in the model itself, but in how you orchestrate it and the data you collect from it.
The LLMs or models are already commoditized i.e. no real MOAT in having the best model alone anymore. Models are surprisingly stupid still (maybe until we solve for real "world" models), agentic harness systems are less so. 
The real competitive advantage is in the harness and the data flywheel. The harness is the system that wraps around the model, and the flywheel is the feedback loop that collects data from users and improves the system over time.

In this post we look at some of the latest and greatest connected to agentic harness engineering and data flywheels, and why this is where the real MOAT is in AI right now. 
We also look at some examples from finance and retail, where the data is dense and the stakes are high, to see how this plays out in practice. Finally, we discuss why vertical SaaS is still a viable play, despite the platform giants' land grab, and what the future holds for AI-powered products.

## The Model Layer Is Commoditizing — Fast

The commoditization of AI models is no longer a prediction — it's measurable fact.

<Callout type="insight" title="The Numbers Tell the Story">

- **Epoch AI**: price to achieve GPT-4-level performance falls **40x/year**; some benchmarks show **900x/year**
- GPT-4 output token pricing dropped **>90%** since March 2023
- DeepSeek R1 matched o1's reasoning for **~$5.6M** training cost (vs $100M+)
- Open-source: **~90% of proprietary capability at ~17x lower cost**
- Quality gap: **15–20 points → 9 points** (Oct 2024 → mid-2025), parity projected Q2 2026
- **37%** of enterprises use 5+ models; **69%** using Google also use OpenAI

</Callout>

Multi-model deployment is now standard operating procedure. Enterprise API spending doubled from **$3.5B to $8.4B** in just six months — even as per-token prices collapsed. Volume is exploding while unit economics race toward zero. The market is voting with its wallet: the model is a commodity input, not a differentiator.

It's not just inference cost that's commoditizing. Reasoning capability itself is following the same curve. OpenAI's o3-mini democratized "PhD-level reasoning" at roughly 15x cheaper than o1. Gartner projects a **90% cost reduction by 2030** for inference on trillion-parameter models. And the provider landscape is fragmenting: Anthropic now commands **32% of enterprise API spend**, surpassing OpenAI which fell from 50% to 25%.

The leaders building these models see it too:

<Citation author="Jared Spataro" source="Microsoft" type="pullquote">
The LLM is the CPU. As powerful CPUs became commodities, the value shifted to the overall system.
</Citation>

- **Sam Altman (OpenAI)**: *"We will maintain less of a lead than we did in previous years."*
- **Satya Nadella (Microsoft)**: *"AI is turning into a commodity we just can't get enough of."*
- **Nandan Nilekani (Infosys)**: *"The models will become more commoditized and the value will switch to the application layer."*

If the model is the CPU, the question becomes: what's the operating system? And who's building it?

## Why the Harness Creates the Moat, Not the Model

The most important insight in AI right now doesn't come from a model paper — it comes from systems thinking.

<Citation author="Matei Zaharia et al." source="Berkeley BAIR Lab, Feb 2024" type="quote">
State-of-the-art AI results are increasingly from compound systems with multiple components, not monolithic models.
</Citation>

Zaharia's team identified four reasons systems beat models: some tasks improve more through design than scaling; systems can be dynamic where models have static training data; systems offer better control and trust; and performance goals vary widely across applications. Andrej Karpathy put it more bluntly: the LLM is the kernel in a larger operating system — tool access, memory, I/O, and code execution are the peripheral layers that create the actual product.

### What Exactly Is the Agentic Harness?

The harness is the comprehensive system infrastructure surrounding an AI model in production:

- **Tool integration** — browsers, terminals, APIs, file systems
- **Context & state management** — memory, session persistence
- **Evaluation frameworks** — continuous output verification against business rules
- **Observability pipelines** — hallucination, drift, failure monitoring
- **Security guardrails** — deterministic validation of probabilistic outputs

A critical architectural distinction: **scaffolding** is what happens pre-execution (system prompt compilation, tool schema registration, subagent setup), while the **harness** is the runtime orchestration layer that wraps the reasoning loop — dispatching tools, managing context, and enforcing safety. The OpenDev paper (2026) formalized this distinction with a five-layer defense-in-depth safety model.

The key insight is the decoupling of probabilistic reasoning from deterministic execution. The LLM ("Cognitive Engine") drafts a probabilistic plan; the "Reasoning Orchestrator" validates it against business rules, access controls, and compliance requirements before anything executes.

### The Evidence Is In

The case for harness engineering moved from theory to empirical fact in 2025–2026:

- **Terminal Bench 2.0**: Claude Opus 4.6 ranked **#33** in its native Claude Code harness but **#5** in a different harness — same model, wildly different results
- **Princeton HAL Leaderboard**: 21,730 agent rollouts across 9 models found the optimal scaffold **flips depending on model family** — Claude needs one scaffold, OpenAI needs a completely different one
- **"Improving 15 LLMs at Coding in One Afternoon"**: harness optimization alone produced dramatic improvements across all models — no retraining required
- **ETH Zurich**: LLM-generated context instructions **hurt performance** while costing 20%+ more tokens; human-written ones gave only ~4% improvement
- **Cursor at $50B**: uses the same underlying models as competitors — differentiation is entirely in the harness

The industry is now split on a revealing debate. Anthropic's Boris Cherny argues *"all the secret sauce is in the model — this is the thinnest possible wrapper."* Jerry Liu of LlamaIndex counters: *"The Model Harness is Everything — the biggest barrier to getting value from AI is your own ability to context and workflow engineer the models."* METR testing found Claude Code and Codex don't significantly outperform basic scaffolds. The data sides with the harness camp: harness engineering is now a recognized discipline, with AIE Europe hosting the world's first Harness Engineering track.

## The Data Flywheel: Your Only Durable Advantage

General internet-scale data is abundant and shared — every model trains on roughly the same corpus. That's not where defensibility lives. What moves the needle is specialized, high-signal data generated in real time inside closed-loop systems that only your product can create.

### Flywheel Building Blocks

- **HITL workflows** — gold-standard data no scraper can replicate
- **Workflow integration** — AI inside EHR/CRM/checkout = you own the context
- **Low-latency feedback** — faster learning = faster competitive separation
- **Proprietary generation** — data that can only exist through your product

### TCO Reduction Through the Flywheel

The flywheel also creates a powerful cost advantage. Organizations start with expensive 70B+ parameter models for complex tasks, then collect successful interactions and curate ground-truth datasets. These feed fine-tuning of smaller models (1B–8B parameters). The result: **a customized 1B model on proprietary data achieves ~96% accuracy of a 70B model** on the same task — at a fraction of the inference cost.

<Callout type="info" title="The Modern Data Moat Has Evolved">
Static data moats are vulnerable — LLMs can generate synthetic approximations. True defensibility comes from owning the software interfaces where data is **naturally and continuously generated**. Capital markets are pricing this in: AI-native vertical SaaS is seeing a **65% funding surge** toward "invisible AI" — embedded intelligence that lives inside existing workflows, not replacement chatbot interfaces.
</Callout>

What does this look like in practice? The strongest evidence comes from two industries where data density is highest: finance and retail.

## Finance Proves the Thesis

Finance is the clearest proof of the harness + flywheel thesis because the data is dense, the stakes are high, and the incumbents have decades of workflow integration that no horizontal player can replicate.

### Bloomberg: The Harness Is the Terminal

Bloomberg invested **~$10M** in BloombergGPT — a 50B parameter model trained on 369B tokens of proprietary financial data. GPT-4 outperformed it on most financial tasks. And yet the Bloomberg Terminal remains completely unassailable: **$28K–$32K/user/year, ~325K subscribers**, with no meaningful churn. The moat isn't the model — it's decades of curated data, 90+ proprietary models, and deep workflow integration. The LLM is a retention feature inside the harness, not the product itself.

### Stripe: Data Flywheel at Scale

- **$1.4T** payment volume (2024), **92%** probability of having seen any given card
- Payments foundation model trained on tens of billions of transactions
- Results: card-testing detection **59% → 97%**, blocked **$917M** in fraud (BFCM 2024), recovered **$6B** in false declines
- Launched Shared Payment Tokens (SPTs) for agentic commerce

### Fin-R1 & NVIDIA

- Fin-R1: 7B model with domain-specific training → surpassed much larger general-purpose models (85.0 ConvFinQA, 76.0 FinQA)
- NVIDIA: Data Flywheel Blueprint orchestrating continuous fine-tuning + evaluation loops

## Why Vertical SaaS Is Still a Play

Despite the platform giants' land grab, vertical SaaS isn't just surviving — it's thriving. The reasons are structural, not sentimental.

### 1. The Data Is the Moat, Not the Feature

A general AI legal assistant sees ~15% churn — it's too broad to be indispensable. A vertical SaaS tool for medical malpractice discovery commands **300% higher pricing with less than 3% churn**. The difference is data depth and workflow specificity.

### 2. Workflow Integration Creates Structural Lock-in

Leaving Procore means ripping out field coordination, compliance documentation, and bid management simultaneously. The AI output is a byproduct of deep integration — and the integration is what creates the switching cost.

### 3. Embedded Financial Infrastructure

Toast and ServiceTitan don't just sell software — they process payments, payroll, and merchant accounts. LLMs can't settle payments, underwrite loans, or interact with banking infrastructure. Revenue flows through **transaction volume**, making these businesses immune to seat-based compression.

### 4. The Numbers

- 2025: verticals = **53% of deal volume**, **30% of capital** ($186B)
- Excluding mega-rounds (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.): **51% of total capital**
- 2026: AI-native vertical SaaS seeing **65% increase** in capital flow
- AI-native Series A medians: **$22M vs $15M traditional** (47% larger rounds)

### 5. Historical Parallel

Despite AWS, Salesforce, and Azure dominating horizontal cloud, vertical SaaS produced Veeva (~$37B peak), Toast, Procore, and ServiceTitan. The **top 20 public vertical SaaS companies hold ~$300B in combined market cap**.

As cloud was maturing, I heard a lot about the hyperscalers actually providing all the security services. Well, that didn't happen.

## Cognitive Debt: The New Technical Debt

There's a cost to getting this wrong. **Cognitive debt** is the AI-era successor to technical debt — and it accumulates silently when enterprises deploy agents without a robust harness.

It manifests as:
- Chronic context loss across interactions
- Uncontrolled and hallucinated tool usage
- Unreliable agent behavior that erodes user trust

<Citation author="Randall Hunt" type="pullquote">
If you don't engineer the harness, you don't get compounding leverage; you get compounding cognitive debt.
</Citation>

The damage extends to human capital too. Junior employees are deskilled by unharnessed AI — they learn to accept outputs without understanding them. A robust harness forces AI to show its work, cite sources, and pause for human-in-the-loop verification. The harness doesn't just protect your product; it protects your team's ability to think.

## The Playbook: Building for the Long Game

Everything in this post distills to five principles. If you're building an AI-powered product, tape these to your monitor.

<Callout type="success" title="Five Principles for Durable AI Advantage">

1. **Own the workflow, not the output** — embed where switching costs are prohibitive
2. **Build the harness first** — orchestration, guardrails, memory, observability; model-agnostic
3. **Spin the flywheel from day one** — every interaction = proprietary data; HITL + eval loops
4. **Compete on integration, not intelligence** — let model labs race on benchmarks; your advantage is domain depth
5. **Price for outcomes, not seats** — value is in the labor line of P&L being replaced

</Callout>

## Conclusion

The model layer is following classic commoditization: initial scarcity, rapid proliferation, price collapse, value migration. We've seen this movie before with CPUs, cloud compute, and storage. The plot doesn't change — only the actors.

Platforms entering verticals face the same old constraint: they cannot go deep enough into domain-specific data loops, regulatory workflows, and integration layers. They'll capture the easy middle, but the edges — where the real money lives — belong to the companies that build the harness and spin the flywheel.

The real competition isn't about who has the best model. It's about who builds the system that gets smarter every day.

<Callout type="insight">
The best AI companies won't have the best models. They'll have systems that get smarter every day — because the harness captures what the model can't.
</Callout>
